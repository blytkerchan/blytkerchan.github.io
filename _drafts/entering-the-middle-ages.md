We are entering the Middle Ages.

We are living in a time when most of the software we use on a daily basis is written by hand using archaic, handcrafted tooling.

The tools, practices, and methods developers use on a daily basis are taught to them by the "masters" of the trade in the modern equivalent of apprenticeships. The modern equivalent of guilds, the engineering orders and associations like the IEEE, the ACM, and the OIQ here in Quebec, set loosely enforced standards, such as the SWEBOK, which are mostly ignored by practitioners.

Our tools are compilers, toolchains, and IDEs, and our programming languages are the wood and nails we mould into useful but vulnerable software. Some of us build the equivalent of battering rams (malware), while others erect the equivalent of castle doors (firewalls) or drawbridges (protocols), but we all use the same tools of the trade in a myriad of bespoke variations.

That is about to change, though. Just as civilization depended on the craftsmanship of the master carpenter until a mere two or three centuries ago (depending on how generous you want to be with carpenters), civilization's dependency on the individual developer's mastery of their trade will wane over the coming few decades.
